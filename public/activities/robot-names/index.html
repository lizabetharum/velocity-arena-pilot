How the math works

Walk through each step. See exactly how algebra picks each piece of your team name.

Step 1 of 4
Start with random seed numbers
The generator picks three integers: x, y, and z. Each is a random whole number between 1 and 10. These are your variables — the raw inputs that drive everything else.
Step 2 of 4
Find the adjective using a linear equation
Plug x and y into a = 2x + y − 1 to get a number. Then use that number to look up word number a in the adjectives list.

This is called a linear equation because neither variable is squared or multiplied together — every term is just a variable times a constant. If you graphed every (x, y) pair that produces the same value of a, you'd get a straight line.

The numbers in front of x and y are called coefficients. They control how much each variable affects the result:
x has coefficient
2
Every time x goes up by 1, a goes up by 2. x has the bigger impact.
y has coefficient
1
Every time y goes up by 1, a goes up by 1. Half the impact of x.
The − 1 at the end is a constant. It shifts every result down by 1 regardless of what x and y are. Without it, the smallest possible output would be 2(1) + 1 = 3. With it, the smallest is 2. That small shift spreads results more evenly across the low end of the list.
Why does the result always land on a real word?

The adjective list has 26 words, numbered 0 to 25. x goes up to 10 and y goes up to 10, so the biggest possible value of a is 2(10) + 10 − 1 = 29. That is too high — index 29 does not exist in the list.

To fix this, the generator uses a mod , which gives the remainder after dividing by . So if a = 29, then 29 mod = , and that index lands on a real word.
Step 3 of 4
Find the animal using substitution
Take the adjective result a and substitute it into b = (a + z) mod . Mod keeps b between 0 and 11 no matter how large a gets. There are exactly 12 animals in the list, so every value of b lands on one.
Step 4 of 4
Build the team number
The team number comes from n = 3z² − x. Square z first, multiply by 3, then subtract x. Larger z values produce bigger numbers, giving elite teams four-digit names.
Key concept: Variables let us write one rule that works for any input. Change x or y and you get a different adjective, but the equation stays the same.
